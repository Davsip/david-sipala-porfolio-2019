About me

I am a curious person who likes to learn from people's behavior, investigate how to meet their needs. I leverage research to design user-centered products and experiences which solve both user and business problems. My process is hands-on, collaborative, and iterative. Although I have a holistic view of user experience design, I am most focused on interaction design, user research, and usability testing. My goal is to design products and experiences that are meaningful and impactful, and I’m especially interested in utilizing new technologies to improve people’s lives in innovative ways.

The Story

I personally had the experience going to restaurants and seen surveys on the receipt, if you fill it out, you get a free reward. But it was too tedious to go to the website and do it. so the idea was to do an app that combines all the restaurant and have a system to do surveys that are fast to do and easy to redeem. !

The idea represented on a Storyboard

The best way to start a project comunicate an idea is with a storyboard. Stories are an effective and inexpensive way to capture, convey and explore experiences in the design process. Storyboards put people at the heart of the design process. They put a human face on analytics data and research findings. Storyboarding is useful for participatory design. Participatory design involves all parties (stakeholders, UI and UX designers, developers, researchers) in the design process, to ensure that the result is as good as possible. With a compelling storyboard that shows how the solution addresses the problem, the product is more likely to be compelling to the target audience.

Competetive Analisys

Many others that are capturing rewards and surveys However, there is currently nothing that streamlines the process for restaurant owners and consumers, the way Chomp Change can. Plenti was funded by American Express and covered loyalty rewards for a multitude of unrelated businesses, such as Kroger and Exxon Mobile. It closed down after many of the larger partners pulled funding due to disappointing ROI. As we researched competitors in this arena, we found options like American Express’s now defunct Plenti, Drop, Surveys on the Go, and many others that are capturing rewards and surveys. However, there is currently nothing that streamlines the process for restaurant owners and consumers, the way Chomp Change can. Plenti was funded by American Express and covered loyalty rewards for a multitude of unrelated businesses, such as Kroger and Exxon Mobile. Purchases at each business earned loyalty points that could then be redeemed at any partner, like Hulu and Lowes. It closed down after disappointing ROI. Many of the larger partners pulled funding due to disappointing ROI.

Findings

We did our own research, with a few local restaurants that offer free items for taking a survey. Currently, after purchase, your receipt allows you to log in online, take a survey, and get a verification code that you have to physically write on your receipt, then save that receipt, remember to take it with you on your next visit, and redeem it, all within a typical 5 - 7 day window. Rarely does anyone do this, yet almost every company has a version of this printed on their receipts

Findings

As we began the concept the idea idea , many questions surfaced: Why Customer don’t customers fill out the surveys? Are companies received valuable feedback from customers? If customer do the surveys, often go un-used, because you lose it Are there any competitors doing a good job with rewards apps?

Workflow

For some of the flows we had to drill down more than a few levels to get a better understanding of the task and how to simplify it. Part of the reason for that was because we were not sure how it would work. We did research by downloading similar apps and using some of our own experiences. We also kept in mind our goal to keep our app easy to use and what will be on each screen. With the guidance of our teachers we went back to simplified the workflows by thinking about what we wanted the users to accomplish on a mobile app and what was really necessary. We realized as a team that we needed to simplify our focus because we have a limited amount of time. We decided to focus on the primary functions of the app. For the consumer side we focused on the existing users login, taking a survey and redeeming a reward. For the business side our user was a Small-Midsize Restaurant owner as an existing user the workflows would be creating a survey and view their analytics.

Screen Design

As we began transitioning to wireframes. We used the Functional Group Worksheet to help use understand functions of the screens we are creating for the app. This helped use to focus on what elements should be primary, secondary, etc. This helped us keep in mind the goal of the screen and the users focus. We created a board in inVision were we could post our sketches and wireframes as we began developing them. We also created an inspiration board brand elements we liked and thought fit the app.

Styleguide

Before we started developing our comps we thought about what we wanted the first impression of our brand to be. During a Skype meeting we came up with several adjectives. Exciting, fun, quick, rewarding. We researched the meaning of color and decided on colors and then chose to us a san serif font because it’s easy to read. We landed on Montserrat a google font. We started to piece our style guide together using a Xd style guide template. We looked at other apps to get inspiration for our brand. We all came up with names for the app and posted them in Trello using a plugin we voted in Trello. There was a few variations of the logo we voted and landed on the one you see here. (Problem Example: Colors, we looked up the color psychology, based off the words we wanted for our brand we chose a color )

Wireframe

Comps for both business and consumer created individually using the style guide we created. We used Google Hangouts to discuss our comps and revised comps. As we reviewed and revised our comps we noticed a few things that could be tested. We created a usability test card in Trello and made a list of things that would be good to usability test.

Personas

From our EPICs and User Stories, we saw common patterns emerge mostly around time saving, consideration and getting free stuff. The final documented Personas were pragmatic style (simplified versions). These consumer personas are what we tested specifically. Then we added several accessibility users. To create our consumer personas, we started with people we knew. We talked to people and found out that these were the most likely combinations that are users would have. David was the inspiration for the techie part of George and then we expanded to the fact that George like gaming and he eats out for lunch every workday. This led us to think about how he could be inspired to use the app and map out plan of the best places to eat for the most reward, even if he ends up sharing the rewards with his friends, he has scored! The initial thought for Thrifty Susie was based on my niece. She is a stay home with four kids (all under 12). She is time crunched and budget conscious, but she always has a great attitude about finding bargains. If she can quickly select a place to drive thru based on rewards available while on the way to drop off the older ones at soccer, she is all-in! And she would really enjoy the peace of sitting alone while her little ones play at the indoor playground with a lunch of chicken nuggets followed by a free ice cream! Our extreme user would be someone who does not ever take time to fill out surveys, but likes getting free stuff when it's easy.

Protype-Usability Test

* We recruited participants based on our consumer personas, and went a step further and found 1 participant matching each of our personas with visual impairments. * Each team member ran their own test and recorded them to share with the team. * Each team member posted our observations, scores, and links to our testing videos via google docs. * Discussed our observations and results, and decided on a list of things to adjust based on common user feedback. It was really hard to not lead the testing participant when he/she didn’t quickly see how to accomplish the task.

Testing-Results

Usability Scores: * We used a scale of 1-7 * Scores were mostly 6’s and 7’s * Everyone saw multiple ways to achieve tasks except for 1 participant who focused on the photos. !
